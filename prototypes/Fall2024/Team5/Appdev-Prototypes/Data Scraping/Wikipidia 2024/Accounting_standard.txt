         <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

         </div>

        <div class="mw-body-content" id="mw-content-text">
         <div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" dir="ltr" lang="en">
          <div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">
           Accounting standards for publicly traded companies
          </div>
          <p>
           Publicly traded companies typically are subject to rigorous standards. Small and midsized businesses often follow more simplified standards, plus any specific disclosures required by their specific lenders and shareholders. Some firms operate on the
           <a href="/wiki/Cash_method_of_accounting" title="Cash method of accounting">
            cash method of accounting
           </a>
           which can often be simple and straightforward. Larger firms most often operate on an
           <a href="/wiki/Basis_of_accounting" title="Basis of accounting">
            accrual basis
           </a>
           . Accrual basis is one of the fundamental accounting assumptions and if it is followed by the company while preparing the Financial statements then no further disclosure is required.
           <b>
            Accounting standards
           </b>
           prescribe in considerable detail what accruals must be made, how the financial statements are to be presented, and what additional disclosures are required.
          </p>
          <p>
           Some important elements that accounting standards cover include identifying the exact entity which is reporting, discussing any "going concern" questions, specifying monetary units, and reporting time frames.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
            <a href="#cite_note-1">
             <span class="cite-bracket">
              [
             </span>
             1
             <span class="cite-bracket">
              ]
             </span>
            </a>
           </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
           In the public sector, 30% of 165 governments surveyed used
           <a href="/wiki/Accrual_accounting_in_the_public_sector" title="Accrual accounting in the public sector">
            accrual accounting
           </a>
           , rather than cash accounting, in 2020.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFACCIPFA_2-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-IFACCIPFA-2">
             <span class="cite-bracket">
              [
             </span>
             2
             <span class="cite-bracket">
              ]
             </span>
            </a>
           </sup>
          </p>
          <meta property="mw:PageProp/toc">
           <div class="mw-heading mw-heading2">
            <h2 id="Limitations">
             Limitations
            </h2>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              [
             </span>
             <a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Limitations">
              <span>
               edit
              </span>
             </a>
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              ]
             </span>
            </span>
           </div>
           <p>
            The notable limitations of accounting standards are their inflexibility, time-consuming process to create them, the difficulty of choosing between alternative treatments and their restrictive scope.
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
             <a href="#cite_note-3">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              3
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
            Accounting standards were largely written in the early 21st century. Massive
            <i>
             accounting irregularities
            </i>
            at large firms such as
            <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worldcom" title="Worldcom">
             Worldcom
            </a>
            and
            <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">
             Enron
            </a>
            illustrate that, despite all these efforts, widespread fraud can still occur, and even be missed by the outside auditors.
           </p>
           <div class="mw-heading mw-heading2">
            <h2 id="Benefits_of_accounting_standards">
             Benefits of accounting standards
            </h2>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              [
             </span>
             <a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Benefits of accounting standards">
              <span>
               edit
              </span>
             </a>
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              ]
             </span>
            </span>
           </div>
           <p>
            The lack of transparent accounting standards in some nations has been cited as increasing the difficulty of doing business in them. In particular, the Asian financial meltdown in the late 1990s has been partially attributed to the lack of detailed accounting standards. Giant firms in some Asian countries were able to take advantage of their poorly devised accounting standards to cover up immense debts and losses, which yielded a collective effect that eventually led the whole region into financial crisis.
           </p>
           <div class="mw-heading mw-heading2">
            <h2 id="Common_accounting_standards_around_the_globe">
             Common accounting standards around the globe
            </h2>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              [
             </span>
             <a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Common accounting standards around the globe">
              <span>
               edit
              </span>
             </a>
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              ]
             </span>
            </span>
           </div>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <a href="/wiki/International_Financial_Reporting_Standards" title="International Financial Reporting Standards">
              International Financial Reporting Standards
             </a>
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            This standard is adopted in whole, or in large part, by many countries. It is acceptable in the U.S. (for a firm located outside of the U.S.) to report in this widely accepted format.
           </p>

             </a>
            </li>
            <li>

             <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grunds%C3%A4tze_ordnungsm%C3%A4%C3%9Figer_Buchf%C3%BChrung" title="de:Grundsätze ordnungsmäßiger Buchführung">

            </li>

             <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
              Turkey
             </a>
             – Uniform Accounting Plan (Turkey)
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
              United Kingdom
             </a>
             –
             <a href="/wiki/Generally_Accepted_Accounting_Practice_(UK)" title="Generally Accepted Accounting Practice (UK)">
              Generally Accepted Accounting Practice (UK)
             </a>
            </li>
            <li>
             <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
              United States
             </a>

             <a href="/wiki/Generally_Accepted_Accounting_Principles_(United_States)" title="Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (United States)">
              Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (United States)
             </a>
             Domestic firms typically report in this format. Foreign firms that trade in the U.S. typically report in IFRS format (below).
            </li>
           </ul>
           <div class="mw-heading mw-heading2">
            <h2 id="Global_standardization_and_IFRS">
             Global standardization and IFRS
            </h2>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              [
             </span>
             <a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting_standard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Global standardization and IFRS">
              <span>
               edit
              </span>
             </a>
             <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
              ]
             </span>
            </span>
           </div>
           <p>
            Many countries use or are converging on the
            <a href="/wiki/International_Financial_Reporting_Standards" title="International Financial Reporting Standards">
             International Financial Reporting Standards
            </a>
            (IFRS) that were established and are maintained by the
            <a href="/wiki/International_Accounting_Standards_Board" title="International Accounting Standards Board">
             International Accounting Standards Board
            </a>
            . In some countries, local accounting principles are applied for regular companies but listed or large companies must conform to IFRS, so statutory reporting is comparable internationally.
           </p>
           <p>
            All listed and grouped EU companies have been required to use IFRS since 2005, Canada moved in 2009,
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
             <a href="#cite_note-5">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              5
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
            Taiwan in 2013,
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
             <a href="#cite_note-6">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              6
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
            and other countries are adopting local versions.
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
             <a href="#cite_note-7">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              7
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
             <a href="#cite_note-8">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              8
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
           </p>
           <p>
            In the
            <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
             United States
            </a>
            , while "... the SEC published a statement of continued support for a single set of high-quality, globally accepted accounting standards, and acknowledged that IFRS is best positioned to serve this role..."
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pwc_9-0">
             <a href="#cite_note-pwc-9">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              9
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
            progress is less evident.
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pwc_9-1">
             <a href="#cite_note-pwc-9">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              9
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
            <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
             <a href="#cite_note-10">
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               [
              </span>
              10
              <span class="cite-bracket">
               ]
              </span>
             </a>
            </sup>
           </p>
